# Food-System Bidirectional Environmental Nexus

Use this reference for reviews that synthesize both directions of the food-environment relationship:

1. how food production drives environmental pressures; and
2. how those environmental changes feed back onto food production, food security, and food-system resilience.

## Running Example

Mogollón et al. (2026), "Broad bidirectional effects of global food production on the environment," Nature Reviews Earth & Environment, is a useful model article for this pattern.

The core workflow lesson is to map food-system evidence as a coupled system rather than a one-way footprint:

- food production increases nutrient imbalance, water use, biodiversity loss, climate forcing, soil-carbon loss, eutrophication, hypoxia, and land-use pressure;
- environmental degradation then feeds back through crop production, livestock systems, and aquatic or blue-food production;
- demand for resource-intensive foods and international trade can shift environmental impacts across borders;
- solutions need both supply-side and demand-side transformations.

## Nexus Map

Organize evidence in two directions.

Food production to environment:

- greenhouse-gas emissions;
- land-use change and deforestation;
- soil carbon loss and soil degradation;
- nutrient imbalance, nitrogen and phosphorus losses;
- eutrophication and hypoxia;
- freshwater withdrawal and irrigation pressure;
- biodiversity loss and pollinator pressure;
- pesticide, antibiotic, and other pollutant burdens;
- aquatic ecosystem pressures from capture fisheries and aquaculture.

Environment to food production:

- extreme heat, drought, flood, and compound extremes affecting crops;
- soil erosion, salinization, nutrient depletion, and soil-health decline;
- water scarcity and irrigation constraints;
- biodiversity and pollinator loss;
- pests, pathogens, weeds, and disease shifts;
- livestock heat stress, disease, and feed-system vulnerability;
- ocean warming, acidification, deoxygenation, eutrophication, and fisheries redistribution;
- regional food-security vulnerability, especially where adaptive capacity is low.

Cross-border and system pathways:

- trade shifts environmental impacts beyond national borders;
- diets and resource-intensive food demand alter land, water, nutrient, and emissions footprints;
- supply chains hide where pressures occur;
- global-average footprints can mask regional vulnerability and local ecological thresholds.

## Workflow

1. Define the food-system boundary: crops, livestock, blue foods, inputs, trade, diets, waste, or full food system.
2. Build a bidirectional pathway map.
3. Separate pressure domains from production receptors.
4. Identify whether each pathway is measured, modeled, reviewed, or inferred.
5. Record the spatial scale: global, national, basin, commodity, supply chain, or farm system.
6. Track whether impacts are production-based or consumption-based.
7. Map vulnerable regions and populations.
8. Separate supply-side, demand-side, circularity, and adaptation strategies.
9. Mark uncertainty, omitted feedbacks, and evidence gaps.
10. Write conclusions as coupled-system claims, not single-footprint claims.

## Strategy Typology

Supply-side:

- nutrient-use efficiency;
- water-use efficiency;
- climate-resilient crop breeding;
- agroecological practices;
- soil-health management;
- circular nutrient and waste flows;
- livestock heat-stress management;
- lower-impact aquaculture and fisheries management.

Demand-side:

- dietary shifts;
- food loss and waste reduction;
- lower resource-intensive food demand;
- sustainable procurement and labelling;
- consumption-based accounting.

System redesign:

- circularity across crop, livestock, and aquatic systems;
- trade accountability;
- producer and consumer responsibility;
- regional adaptation planning;
- policy packages that reduce pressure without displacing it elsewhere.

## Guardrails

- Do not reduce the review to a one-way footprint table if feedbacks are central.
- Do not compare food groups only per calorie when nutrient density, protein, livelihood, and cultural functions matter for the claim.
- Do not treat trade as impact reduction unless displacement and leakage are tracked.
- Do not use global averages to make local food-security claims without regional vulnerability evidence.
- Do not present supply-side efficiency as sufficient if demand growth, rebound effects, or absolute pressure limits are not addressed.
- Do not conflate adaptation strategies with mitigation strategies; some strategies do both, but the pathway should be explicit.
