# Second-Order Meta-Analysis Decision Rules

Use this reference when deciding whether to statistically synthesize existing reviews/meta-analyses.

## Step 1: Classify the Project

- **Umbrella review**: summarizes multiple reviews/meta-analyses, often narratively or by evidence grading.
- **Second-order meta-analysis**: statistically synthesizes review-level/meta-analytic estimates.
- **Review of reviews**: may be broader and not necessarily quantitative.

Do not use these labels interchangeably.

## Step 2: Check Primary-Study Overlap

Build a primary-study by review matrix.

Do not pool review-level effects naively when the same primary studies appear in multiple reviews.

## Step 3: Check Review Quality

Use AMSTAR 2-style logic for systematic-review quality where suitable:

- distinguish critical from non-critical weaknesses;
- avoid creating a simple total score;
- interpret confidence categories cautiously.

Use ROBIS-style logic when the question is risk of bias in the review itself.

## Step 4: Check Effect Compatibility

Review-level estimates must be comparable:

- same outcome construct;
- same effect metric or defensible conversion;
- similar inclusion scope;
- similar population or exposure definition;
- transparent model assumptions.

## Step 5: Choose Synthesis Type

Prefer narrative umbrella synthesis when:

- overlap is high;
- effect metrics differ;
- reviews use incompatible eligibility criteria;
- review quality is highly variable;
- older reviews are superseded.

Consider second-order pooling only when:

- review-level effects are compatible;
- overlap is quantified;
- dependence is modeled or sensitivity-tested;
- interpretation makes clear that the unit is a review/meta-analysis, not an independent primary study.
